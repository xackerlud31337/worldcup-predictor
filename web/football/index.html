      How this predictor works

      Under the hood this site runs a Dixon-Coles model: a pair of Poisson goal models, one per team, fitted on international results from 1990 to today. Every past match shapes each team's attack and defence rating, but not equally: recent games and big tournaments count far more than ancient friendlies, thanks to an exponential time decay (a result loses half its influence every four years) and per-competition weights (a World Cup match matters roughly twice as much as a friendly). From those ratings the model computes how many goals each side is expected to score, builds a probability grid over every possible scoreline (with the Dixon-Coles correction that fixes the plain Poisson model's blind spot on low-scoring draws) and then runs thousands of Monte-Carlo simulations to produce the win/draw/loss percentages and most likely scorelines you see. For teams with little recent data the estimate is blended with an Elo-rating prior, so one obscure fixture can't produce a wild prediction. Everything runs locally in your browser, no server involved.

      What the tags mean

      Expected goals (xG home / xG away)
      The model's average goals per match for each side: if this matchup were played many times, this is what each team would score on average. A 1.8–0.9 reading is not a score prediction; it says team 1 creates about twice team 2's scoring output.
      Use xG data
      Switches to team strengths fitted with StatsBomb expected-goals numbers blended into the actual results. xG measures the quality of chances created rather than just goals scored, which is often a better signal of how good a team really is than the final scoreline.
      When to use which: turn xG on when you want the fairest read on which team is genuinely stronger, since it filters out finishing luck. Turn it off after pressing Refit latest: the refit updates only the standard model, so with xG off your prediction includes the very newest results.
      ⟳ Refit latest
      Downloads the newest international results (~4 MB) and re-fits the entire model right in your browser, so matches played since this site was published are taken into account. Takes a few seconds; applies to the standard model (the xG bundle keeps its original fit).
      Dixon-Coles vs Skellam
      Dixon-Coles evaluates the full scoreline matrix with the low-score correction, the accurate choice. Skellam approximates using only the distribution of the goal difference; slightly faster, slightly cruder.
      Most likely scorelines
      Exact probabilities computed from the model's full scoreline matrix, not random samples, so the same matchup with the same settings always shows the same numbers. In football the top scorelines are often nearly tied at around 10% each; no single final score is ever truly "likely".
      Betting view: fair odds
      Each market's model probability converted to decimal odds (1 ÷ probability), the break-even price. Bookmakers add a margin, so their odds on "safe" favourites are usually below fair value; a bet is only mathematically good when their offered odds are higher than the fair odds here. Markets settle on the 90-minute result: in knockout games a win bet loses if the team only wins in extra time. The model knows nothing about lineups or injuries. Not betting advice.
      Victory margin
      The same probabilities regrouped by the final gap in goals instead of the exact score: "how big will the difference be?" rather than "exactly which scoreline?". Like dice: rolling exactly 4+3 is rare, but a total of 7 is the most common sum, because many combinations produce it. Likewise a team's "win by 2+" bundles 2-0, 3-0, 3-1, 4-1… into one group that can be the most likely outcome even though each exact score looks small alone. Works for any matchup; between equals, "draw" and "by 1" simply become the big bars.
      Team 1 at home
      Applies the fitted home-advantage boost (roughly 25% more goals) to team 1. Leave it off for a neutral venue, as in most World Cup matches.
