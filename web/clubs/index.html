      How this predictor works

      Under the hood this page runs the same Dixon-Coles model as the international predictor, but fitted on club football: nine seasons of the Premier League, La Liga, Bundesliga, Serie A and Ligue 1 (plus six feeder leagues), together with every Champions League match in that window. The Champions League games matter twice: they sharpen each club's rating and they are the only matches where clubs from different leagues meet, so they are what puts Arsenal and Bayern on one comparable scale. Because club squads change every transfer window, results lose half their influence per year (the international model uses four): the ratings live in the present. From each club's attack and defence rating the model computes expected goals, builds a probability grid over every scoreline (with the Dixon-Coles low-score correction) and reads off the win/draw/loss odds, margins and fair odds. Clubs with little recent top-level data are blended toward an Elo prior. In back-testing on the most recent season it beat that Elo baseline on every metric, most clearly in the Champions League. Everything runs locally in your browser, no server involved.

      What the tags mean

      Expected goals (xG home / xG away)
      The model's average goals per match for each side: if this matchup were played many times, this is what each club would score on average. A 1.8–0.9 reading is not a score prediction; it says team 1 creates about twice team 2's scoring output.
      Team 1 at home
      Applies the fitted home-advantage boost (roughly 24% more goals). Club matches are almost never on neutral ground, so it's on by default; turn it off for a cup final on neutral turf.
      Dixon-Coles vs Skellam
      Dixon-Coles evaluates the full scoreline matrix with the low-score correction, the accurate choice. Skellam approximates using only the distribution of the goal difference; slightly faster, slightly cruder.
      Most likely scorelines
      Exact probabilities computed from the model's full scoreline matrix, not random samples, so the same matchup with the same settings always shows the same numbers. In football the top scorelines are often nearly tied at around 10% each; no single final score is ever truly "likely".
      Betting view: fair odds
      Each market's model probability converted to decimal odds (1 ÷ probability), the break-even price. Bookmakers add a margin, so their odds on "safe" favourites are usually below fair value; a bet is only mathematically good when their offered odds are higher than the fair odds here. Markets settle on the 90-minute result: in knockout games a win bet loses if the team only wins in extra time. The model knows nothing about lineups or injuries. Not betting advice.
      Victory margin
      The same probabilities regrouped by the final gap in goals instead of the exact score: "how big will the difference be?" rather than "exactly which scoreline?". A club's "win by 2+" bundles 2-0, 3-0, 3-1, 4-1… into one group that can be the most likely outcome even though each exact score looks small alone.
      Squads & availability
      Each player carries a rating built from his non-penalty expected goals + expected assists per 90 minutes over the last two seasons (recent season counting double), shrunk toward his position's average so short stints don't produce absurd numbers. A club's baseline adds up its players' ratings weighted by how much they actually play, and by construction that matches the club's team rating — so with everyone available, the player layer changes nothing. Unticking a player replaces him with a bench-level stand-in and rescales the club's attack; missing defenders and goalkeepers instead concede a little extra to the opponent (a flat approximation — defensive value can't be measured from xG). Player data covers the big-five leagues, so clubs from other countries show no squad list. Ratings reflect last season's squads until the new season's data starts accumulating.
      Cross-league matchups
      Comparing clubs from different leagues (say Liverpool vs Bayern) leans on the Champions League games linking those leagues. That works well for regular European participants; for clubs that rarely play in Europe the comparison is rougher — the model will tell you when its data is thin.
